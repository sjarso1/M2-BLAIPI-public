M2-BLAIPI

Metabolic Pathway Modeling to Support Risk Stratification in Burkitt Lymphoma

Research

M2-BLAIPI studies whether biologically informed metabolic features can strengthen risk stratification in Burkitt lymphoma beyond what clinical variables alone provide.

Clinical Need

Burkitt lymphoma is an aggressive B-cell malignancy and a major cause of childhood cancer mortality in malaria-endemic regions of sub-Saharan Africa. Despite cost-effective chemotherapy regimens, survival in low- and middle-income countries remains substantially lower than in high-income settings. The Burkitt Lymphoma International Prognostic Index (BL-IPI) is built on clinical variables and therefore does not reflect the biological heterogeneity that contributes to differing outcomes.

Scientific Background

Recent genomic work has shown that Burkitt lymphoma can be divided into distinct genetic subgroups defined by recurrent mutation patterns affecting MYC signaling, B-cell receptor signaling, chromatin remodeling, and tumor suppressor regulation. These subgroups suggest that the disease represents several biologically distinct states rather than a single uniform entity. Tumor metabolism is a downstream functional consequence of these oncogenic programs, which makes metabolic pathway activity a candidate bridge between molecular alterations and clinical behavior.

Research Questions

  • Are specific metabolic pathway activity patterns associated with Burkitt lymphoma genetic subgroups?
  • Can metabolic and molecular features add prognostic information beyond the clinical variables used in BL-IPI?
  • How should endemic context be represented when modeling risk in settings where Burkitt lymphoma is most common?

Research Aims

  1. Pathway signatures. Identify metabolic pathway signatures associated with genetic subgroups.
  2. Feature engineering. Develop literature-derived metabolic features and composite scores linking oncogenic drivers to metabolic phenotypes.
  3. Risk modeling. Develop and evaluate a multivariate risk-modeling framework, benchmarked against established clinical prognostic tools.

Project Significance

Metabolic pathway features may offer a biologically interpretable way to improve on clinical-only prognostic tools, and integrating endemic context is important for the populations most affected by the disease. The project is designed as open and reproducible so that its methods can be reviewed, reused, and extended by other researchers.

Long-Term Translation Vision

The long-term aspiration is a decision-support framework that could help oncologists in low- and middle-income countries, integrate with existing staging systems, and contribute to reducing survival disparities in endemic settings. This vision describes the project's direction; it is not a claim of present clinical readiness.
